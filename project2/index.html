1. Overfitting

I noticed a massive shift when I dropped the hidden_size from 256 to 128. In my initial runs with 256 units, the validation perplexity exploded to around 15k(not included in the report). I think this happened because the model had too much capacity that it was using those extra neurons to memorize the exact sequence of the training text rather than learning any actual structure of the language. By simplifying the architecture to 128 units, the model was forced to generalize a bit more, which brought the validation numbers down to a lower range.

2. Embedding Bottleneck

Even with better regularization (dropout and clipping), the model never quite converged. Even though I realised it quite late; I believe the embedding dimension (64) was a major bottleneck. When you move from character-level to word-level modeling, your vocabulary size jumps from ~27 characters to thousands of unique words,(37091 was the number of total word tokens) . Trying to map the meaning of thousands of words into a tiny 64-dimensional space was definitely the biggest reason for having this result.

3. Word Sparsity:

I do suspect the simple RNN also couldn't see the rare words often enough to learn where they belong. Without the gating mechanisms of something more modern like a GRU or LSTM, the simple RNN likely forgot the beginning of the sentence by the time it reached the end, leaving it stuck at a perplexity plateau.

Task 2:

1.Gradient Clipping:

When I trained the model without gradient clipping, the validation perplexity reached 473.8, which is very close to the results achieved with clipping enabled. This suggests that in this specific configuration, the model did not encounter significant exploding gradients. Gradient clipping acts primarily as a safety measure; if the gradients naturally stay within a reasonable range during backpropagation, the clipping mechanism remains inactive and does not alter the training outcome.

ReLU:

In theory, replacing Tanh with ReLU should help mitigate vanishing gradients. However, this makes the model more susceptible to exploding gradients since the activations are unbounded.

My experimental results were counterintuitive:

Without clipping: 4276.30 perplexity

With clipping: 4588.52 perplexity

Ideally, clipping should improve or stabilize the ReLU model, but I observed the opposite. This suggests that the gradient clipping may have acted as a bottleneck. By using a clipping threshold of 1, I might have been too restrictive, essentially blocking the optimizer in a way and preventing it from taking the large steps necessary to navigate the steep loss landscape created by the ReLU. However, I am not completely sure of the reason behind it.

Hidden Units: The results show that a learning rate of 1.0 significantly outperforms 0.5 for both hidden layer sizes, indicating that 0.5 might be too low, leading to slower convergence. While a larger hidden size (512) achieves slightly lower training perplexity compared to 256 at lr=1.0, it also leads to a slightly higher validation perplexity, suggesting a  generalization capacity within the given epochs. Overall, hiddens=256, lr=1.0 appears to be the most effective configuration among those tested, yielding the best balance between training and validation performance with the lowest validation perplexity.

Task 2: 

Quantitative Performance: The training logs show that the "Reset Only" variant initially converges more quickly in early epochs but eventually plateaus near the same level as the “Update only. In contrast, the "Update Only" variant shows more stable, long-term improvement, which aligns with the theoretical role of the Update Gate in managing long-term dependencies.

Reset Only: The output shows severe repetition ("the machine I stood of the strange of the strange of the strange..."). Without the Update Gate, the model lacks a mechanism to steadily progress the hidden state, causing it to get stuck in smaller loops.

Update Only: The output is significantly more coherent and varied ("i had see the time traveller and..."). This shows that the Update Gate is more vital for maintaining long-term context and grammatical structure, as it allows the model to "carry over" information across more time steps.

Run 5 achieved the highest BLEU score (0.0930), suggesting that a moderate number of layers (4) paired with a relatively higher learning rate (0.002) and significant dropout (0.3) provided the best balance between model complexity and generalization for this dataset.

Overfitting: Deeper models (e.g., Run 4 with 6 layers) did not necessarily outperform Run 5, likely due to the small size of the training set (600 examples), which makes complex models prone to overfitting.

Regularization: Higher dropout rates (0.3 in Runs 4 and 5) generally led to better BLEU scores compared to runs with low or no dropout (Run 1), indicating that regularization is crucial to prevent the model from simply memorizing the training pairs.

Task 2:

Comparison of Results:

Final BLEU Score: The masked version significantly outperforms the unmasked version. When masks are not applied, the model often produces sequences filled with <pad> tokens, leading to a near-zero or significantly lower BLEU score.

Convergence: The unmasked model appears to "converge" to a low loss faster, but this is deceptive. It is merely learning to predict the <pad> tokens which dominate the padded sequences.

Perplexity: The perplexity for the unmasked model is often lower because the majority of the tokens it predicts (the padding) are correct and easy to guess, but this does not translate to translation quality.

The perplexity (PPL) curves for the unmasked models show a deceptive, rapid convergence, appearing much smoother and more stable than the masked versions. However, this is not indicative of superior learning; rather, it reflects a statistical bias toward padding.
