PART 1

This report analyzes the results of hyperparameter tuning for an attention-based Neural Machine Translation (NMT) model and visualizes the attention weights to understand the model's focus during translation.

Top Performing Configurations

Based on the experimental runs, the configurations that achieved the best performance are summarized below:

  • Config 1 (Best BLEU: 0.1000): 32 Embedding size, 32 Hidden units, 1 Layer, 0.0 Dropout, 0.005 Learning Rate.

Achieved the best BLEU score (0.1000) among all runs, despite showing signs of overfitting (Train PPL 1.13 vs. Valid PPL 2.40). This suggests that for this small dataset and limited num_steps, a simpler model with fewer layers might perform better, or that the BLEU score might be a bit misleading here given the high validation perplexity.

  • Config 3 (BLEU: 0.0756): 32 Embedding size, 32 Hidden units, 6 Layers, 0.2 Dropout, 0.001 Learning Rate.

Achieved the lowest validation perplexity (2.20) among all runs, suggesting the best generalization, but a mid-range BLEU score (0.0756). With num_layers=6, a moderate dropout of 0.2 and a lower learning rate of 0.001 likely helped in regularizing the deeper model and allowed for more stable convergence. The lower learning rate allows the model to explore the loss landscape more carefully. This combination resulted in the best generalization performance as indicated by the validation perplexity.

  • Config 5 (BLEU: 0.0500): 64 Embedding size, 64 Hidden units, 4 Layers, 0.3 Dropout, 0.002 Learning Rate.

The results are consistent with a model that is learning without severe overfitting or underfitting, but not achieving the best specific metric due to the difficulty of the task or limitations of the dataset.

Feature Impact and Performance Drivers

The results highlight several key factors that influenced the model's ability to generalize:

  • Model Depth vs. Overfitting: Given the small num_examples (1000) and num_steps (10). Models with fewer layers (Run 1) or proper regularization (Run 3) showed better PPL, but BLEU scores didn't always directly correlate, potentially due to the short nature of the sentences.
  • Dropout: Without it (like Run 1), even a simple model overfits. With too many layers (Run 2), dropout needs to be tuned carefully.
  • Learning Rate Sensitivity: A higher learning rate (0.005) allowed simpler models to converge quickly, but deeper models required more careful tuning to avoid exploding or vanishing gradients during the 200-epoch training cycle.

Task 2: Attention Visualization

For attention visualization, we re-trained Run 3 (num_layers=6, dropout=0.2, lr=0.001) as it demonstrated the best generalization performance (lowest validation perplexity). We then translated several English sentences into French and visualized the attention weights as heatmaps. Due to the limited dataset and model complexity, the translations are often incorrect or incomplete, but we can still observe some patterns.

Findings from Visualization

By visualizing the attention weights as heatmaps, the following observations were made:

  • In successful translations, the heatmap typically shows a diagonal or near-diagonal pattern. This indicates that the model is correctly focusing on "I" when generating "Je,".
  • Even when the translation is wrong like turning "She" into "je",  the model is still trying to find a connection between the words. The attention mechanism might be looking at the right part of the English sentence, but the rest of the model isn't strong enough to turn that focus into a correct French sentence.

The Mysterious Translation of <eos> to "je"

It was observed that frequently, the <eos> (end-of-sequence) token is translated to "je" (I) by the model, even after manually verifying that the vocabulary mapping for <eos> is correct. the decoder learns to predict <eos> when it determines that the target sentence is complete. If the model hasn't seen enough diverse examples of sentence endings, or if the internal hidden states don't consistently capture the end-of-sentence signal, it will struggle to generate <eos> appropriately. Additionally, "je" (I) is an extremely common word in French, especially at the beginning of sentences. When the model is uncertain about what to generate next, perhaps because its internal state is ambiguous or the attention weights are diffuse; it might fall back to predicting a highly frequent token like "je".

I experimented with several configurations, varying parameters such as embed_size, num_hiddens, num_layers, dropout, lr, and crucially, num_heads. Two sets of configurations were primarily tested: a baseline set with a single head (single_head_configs) and another set explicitly exploring multiple heads (multi_head_configs).

Training and Validation Perplexity:

We tracked the training and validation perplexity for each model over 200 epochs. The perplexity plots visually represent how well the models generalize to unseen data.

  • Single-Head Models (e.g., '1-MHA-1', '5-MHA-1'): While training perplexity generally showed a decreasing trend, indicating learning, validation perplexity exhibited more varied behavior across single-head models. For instance, '1-MHA-1' and '5-MHA-1' showed an increase in validation perplexity towards later epochs, suggesting potential overfitting, while others like '3-MHA-1' and '4-MHA-1' showed a decreasing trend. However, even for those with decreasing validation perplexity, some still struggled, exhibiting higher perplexity values, possibly due to increased complexity (more layers, higher dropout) without the benefits of multi-head attention to handle the added capacity efficiently for our limited dataset.

  • Multi-Head Models (e.g., '1-MHA-2', '1-MHA-4', '1-MHA-8'): These models often demonstrated more stable perplexity curves, especially in validation. For instance, the '1-MHA-4' configuration (4 heads) showed a promising balance, indicating effective learning and generalization. The perplexity curves for multi-head models generally indicate that the models are able to learn more robust representations, preventing overfitting or underfitting to the training data as effectively as some of the less-performing single-head models. The plot_perplexity function clearly illustrates these trends, with different lines representing the train and validation perplexities for each run.

BLEU Score:

  • Single-Head Performance: The best single-head model was 1-MHA-1, achieving a BLEU score of 0.1575. Some other single-head configurations (2-MHA-1, 3-MHA-1, 4-MHA-1) performed significantly worse, reaching BLEU scores of 0.0256. This highlights the sensitivity of simpler attention mechanisms to hyperparameter choices and model complexity on a relatively small dataset.

  • Multi-Head Performance: When we introduced multi-head attention, we saw a notable improvement in some cases. For instance, the 1-MHA-4 configuration (with 4 heads, based on a similar base as the 5-MHA-1 single-head model) achieved a BLEU score of 0.1936. This is a significant improvement over the best single-head model (1-MHA-1 at 0.1575), and even more so compared to the 5-MHA-1 model (0.0973), which shares more similar base configuration parameters.

  • Effect of Number of Heads: The number of heads plays a crucial role. For the 1-MHA- series, 4 heads (1-MHA-4: 0.1936) outperformed 2 heads (1-MHA-2: 0.1529) and 8 heads (1-MHA-8: 0.1831). A similar trend was observed in the 5-MHA- series, where 4 heads (5-MHA-4: 0.1705) again yielded the best results among its multi-head counterparts. This suggests that while multi-head attention can significantly boost performance, there's an optimal number of heads for a given model architecture and dataset size. Too few heads might not capture enough diverse information, while too many could lead to redundancy, increased computational cost, or difficulty in training due to parameter explosion, especially with a limited dataset.

Task 1:

The implemented Transformer model, configured with specified hyperparameters (e.g., num_hiddens=32, num_layers=2, num_heads=4, dropout=0.1), underwent training for 10 epochs on an English-to-French translation task. The training and validation loss trajectories indicate a healthy learning process, with both metrics consistently decreasing:

Final Training Loss: 0.119

Final Validation Loss: 0.101

Average BLEU Score on Validation Set: 0.13

The convergence of both training and validation losses suggests that the model is effectively learning the underlying patterns in the data and generalizing reasonably well to unseen examples. The BLEU score of 0.13 provides a quantitative measure of translation quality, serving as a baseline.

Task 2:

a) Ablation of Positional Encoding

The Positional Encoding part was removed from both the encoder and decoder components, meaning the model's input embeddings did not receive information about the relative or absolute position of tokens within a sequence.

Observations:

Final Training Loss: 0.146 (Increased from baseline)

Final Validation Loss: 0.143 (Increased from baseline)

Average BLEU Score on Validation Set: 0.03 (Significant drop from baseline)

The ablation of positional encoding resulted in a dramatic deterioration of translation quality, with the BLEU score plummeting from 0.13 to a mere 0.03. This severe decline underscores the critical role of positional encoding in Transformer architectures. Without positional information, the model processes tokens as an unordered set, effectively losing the syntactic and semantic context derived from the order. While the training process still showed some reduction in loss, the inability to correctly interpret sequential relationships made the translation even worse.

b) Ablation of Self-Attention

In this experiment, the self-attention mechanisms within both the encoder blocks and the decoder's first attention layer were deactivated.

Observations:

Final Training Loss: 0.188 (Increased from baseline)

Final Validation Loss: 0.173 (Increased from baseline)

Average BLEU Score on Validation Set: 0.20 (Increased from baseline)

This result presents an intriguing and somewhat counter-intuitive finding. Disabling self-attention led to an increase in the average BLEU score from 0.13 to 0.20, while simultaneously yielding higher training and validation losses compared to the baseline.

It's possible that a model with higher cross-entropy loss might still produce outputs that align better with human judgment of translation quality (as captured by BLEU) for this specific dataset. Another reason for relatively small datasets or less complex tasks, highly expressive mechanisms like self-attention can sometimes overfit or introduce noise making the BLEU score worse.

Comparing all of them, the best BLEU score is achieved from the transformer without positional encoding and the second best(mentioning it s it is very close to the best one) is achieved from the 1-MHA-4.
